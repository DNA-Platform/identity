Synthesized consensus on unsolved computational complexity problem
It remains formally unresolved — P versus NP is the central open problem in complexity theory and carries a Clay Millennium Prize precisely because no proof exists either way. The strong consensus among theorists (Gasarch's surveys put it around 80–90%) is that P ≠ NP, and the best evidence for that is structural rather than empirical: if P = NP the polynomial hierarchy collapses, one-way functions and most modern cryptography become impossible, and decades of intense effort have failed to find a polynomial algorithm for any of thousands of NP-complete problems despite enormous incentive. The honest caveat is that we also have proof that this is genuinely hard to settle — the relativization (Baker–Gill–Solovay), natural proofs (Razborov–Rudich), and algebrization barriers each rule out broad classes of techniques, which is why no separation has gone through. The case for P = NP is comparatively thin, resting mainly on historical humility (problems like primality and linear programming turned out easier than expected), so "P ≠ NP but unproven" is the defensible position rather than a settled fact.
