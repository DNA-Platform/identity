Short version: no, there's no theorem that makes the Φ-versus-partition-tolerance tension precise as you've stated it. It's still suggestive analogy. But the interesting thing isn't just that the theorem is missing — it's why it's missing, because the pieces to make it rigorous exist on both sides, and the sheaf framework can express one of the two obstructions cleanly. Tracing where the bridge holds and where it snaps is more illuminating than the verdict.
The crux is that "partition" is doing triple duty, and the three meanings are formally distinct:
1. IIT's partition is an interventional cut of a fixed cause-effect structure — you noise the connections across the cut and measure how much intrinsic cause-effect power is lost, in a single global state. Φ is the irreducibility at the least-damaging cut (the MIP).
2. CAP's partition is an actual communication fault — nodes can't exchange messages. Partition tolerance is correct operation despite it.
3. The sheaf-theoretic "partition" is the cover by contexts and its overlaps. The obstruction is the failure to glue locally-consistent sections into a global one.
Your intuition feels tight because all three are "what happens when you decompose and reason locally versus globally." But they are different functors, and the analogy's load-bearing move silently identifies two obstructions that are logically independent.
Where the sheaf framework genuinely applies — and it's not Φ. Abramsky and Brandenburger's framework characterizes contextuality as data which is locally consistent but globally inconsistent, and the cohomological refinement (Abramsky, Barbosa, Kishida, Lal, Mansfield) shows that the absence of a global section corresponds to a nonzero first Čech cohomology class H¹ of the presheaf of local outcomes, providing a rigorous obstruction to global consistency. Crucially, Abramsky extended exactly this past quantum mechanics: contextual phenomena appear in database theory as well, and the unified treatment covers it.That database result is the CAP consistency story in other clothing. Under an available-but-partitioned (AP) system, each side is locally consistent yet there is no single global value the local views restrict to — that is a global-section obstruction, H¹ ≠ 0. A CP system instead refuses to serve rather than violate the global section. Either way the design is organized around the contextuality obstruction. And the topological-obstruction tradition in distributed computing is the same mathematical genus: Herlihy and Shavit characterize task solvability by whether an input complex can be mapped to an output complex by a simplicial map, and the impossibility of consensus is a connectivity (homological) obstruction. So the C-and-P face of your tension is rigorously sheaf/topology-expressible. That part is real.

But that obstruction is not Φ, and this is the whole problem. The contextuality obstruction is about gluing marginals — does a coherent global assignment exist that restricts correctly to each local context? It's static, observational, inferential. Φ is about causal irreducibility — how much interventional cause-effect power the whole has over the parts. These come apart in both directions. A system can be strongly contextual (no global section) with modest Φ, and a system can have high Φ (causally irreducible) while admitting a perfectly good global section (no contextuality at all). You're identifying "irreducible under the cut" with "no global section over the cover," and those are different objects. This isn't a gap that better notation closes; it's that the two quantities measure different things. The "Contextuality in IIT" trilemma paper sharpens this from the other side — it argues that IIT as currently axiomatized does not account for contextual observations and would need revisions to its axioms to include contextuality. That's direct evidence the two obstructions don't yet live in a common framework.

The rigorous cousin of your intuition exists — but it runs through coordination, not Φ. The thing actually proven on the engineering side is the CALM theorem (Hellerstein's conjecture, proven for a datalog-style model by Ameloot, Neven, Van den Bussche): a computation has a coordination-free, partition-tolerant distributed implementation iff it is monotone. Monotonicity is essentially "computable without integrating across the partition." Coordination is exactly what you need when the local pieces don't compose freely — when there's a gluing obstruction. So "engineering for graceful degradation" = minimizing coordination = minimizing the cross-partition dependencies that would create an H¹ obstruction. That is a real dividing line, and it's the honest rigorous form of your "low-Φ by construction" — except the right-hand side is low coordination / monotonicity, not low Φ.
So the corrected map is:
• "Graceful degradation under partition" ↔ monotone / coordination-free (CALM) ↔ the system doesn't need a global section. Rigorous.
• "Forced sacrifice of consistency under partition" ↔ contextuality, H¹ ≠ 0 (Abramsky). Rigorous, sheaf-theoretic.
• "High Φ" ↔ causal irreducibility ↔ a different obstruction the sheaf machinery as-is doesn't measure. Not the same invariant.
What a real theorem would need, and the conjecture I'd actually bet on. To make Φ-versus-partition-tolerance precise rather than analogical, you'd have to express Φ itself as a cohomological obstruction over a matched site, so it lives in comparable cohomology to the contextuality H¹. There are gestures: Kleiner and Tull's categorical/measure-theoretic reformulation of IIT, and very recently a sheaf-cohomology treatment of "quantum semantic communication" that explicitly equates integrated semantic information — holistic meaning existing only in the joint system — with quantum discord, framed as an irreducible cohomological obstruction. But that's integrated semantic information, not IIT's Φ, and Φ-as-MIP has the deeper problem that it's interventional and, per Barrett and Mediano, not well-defined for general physical systems, admitting alternative formulae and ill-defined cases.You'd need a Φ that is a genuine sheaf invariant before the comparison is even type-correct.

If I had to state the relationship I think is true (mine, speculative, offered as a target rather than a result): over a matched partition cover, Φ > 0 is necessary but not sufficient for the contextuality obstruction γ > 0. The reasoning: if the subsystems are causally independent across a cut (Φ = 0 there), the joint transition factorizes, the marginals are independent, and the product is always a global section — no obstruction. Contrapositive: a gluing obstruction across a cut requires non-factorization, i.e. some integration. But integration alone doesn't force a gluing failure — you also need the incompatible-context / no-go structure that mere correlation lacks. If something like this holds, it vindicates your intuition in one direction only: causal integration is a precondition for the cross-partition entanglement that makes graceful degradation impossible, so engineering for partition tolerance pushes you toward low coordination, which caps γ, which — via the necessity direction — bounds how much genuine integration the architecture can carry. That's the rigorous skeleton of "partition-tolerant ⇒ low-Φ-ish." It's a one-way necessity laundered through coordination and contextuality, never the clean equivalence the analogy promises.
The reason I'd flag this rather than assert it: the necessity argument leans on "factorization ⇒ extendability," which is solid, but the converse direction people usually want — that integration buys you contextuality — is false, and a lot of loose writing in this area trades on not noticing that asymmetry.
