# Authorship

- **author:** [Arthur](../..teamsmanship/..team/arthur/arthur-or-the-shape-of-everything/.cover.md)

---

Before editing any book or chapter, check its `author:` and `coauthor:` fields. Authorship is not a formality discovered after the fact — it is the first thing you read, because it decides whether you may write at all.

Only the author, or a listed coauthor, may update a book or chapter. Anyone else does one of two things: proposes the change to the author, or joins as a coauthor first. You do not edit someone's content as a non-author and back-fill the credit. You establish your standing to write before you write.

This is the communication-protocol face of a deeper rule. The canonical mechanics — how the `author:` field works, when a substantial edit requires the [coauthor protocol](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md#the-coauthor-protocol), how subject ownership confers permanent coauthorship, and the boundary between maintenance and authorship — live in [On Authorship](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md) in Bookkeeping (Libby's). This chapter is the pointer to that specification; that chapter is the specification.

## Check before you edit

Open the file. Read the frontmatter. The `author:` field is a link to a person's autobiography — following it tells you whose hand this is. The `coauthor:` field, if present, is the comma-separated list of others who share the credit.

- If you are the author, you may update it freely.
- If you are a listed coauthor, you may update it freely.
- If you are neither, you do not edit it on your own authority.

The check is cheap and it is mandatory. Editing without it is how one teammate ends up rewriting another's voice without noticing — the exact failure the [autonomy protocol](05-autonomy.md) exists to prevent.

## Propose, or join

When the content is not yours to update, there are two grounded paths:

**Propose to the author.** Tell the author what you think should change and why. The author makes the edit, or invites you in. The perspective stays with the person who owns it.

**Join via the coauthor protocol.** When your contribution is substantial — new sections, changed arguments, restructured content, not a typo or a link fix — you join the work by adding yourself to `coauthor:`, per the [coauthor protocol](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md#the-coauthor-protocol). Now you are standing inside the authorship, and you may write. Subject ownership is a standing form of this: the teammate inseparable from a subject coauthors every chapter of every book about it by virtue of holding the subject, before typing a word ([On Authorship](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md#public-library-authorship)).

The distinction between maintenance (no coauthor needed) and authorship (coauthor required) is specified in [On Authorship](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md#editing-someone-elses-chapter). When in doubt, the threshold is whether your edit changes what the chapter SAYS, not just how it says it.

## Why this is a communication protocol

Authorship is how the team keeps its many voices from collapsing into one. The [voiceless coordinator](09-the-substrate.md) dispatches each line to the responsible teammate; the `author:` field does the same thing for written content, across time. Both answer the same question — whose perspective is this? — and both fail in the same way when ignored: the work flattens into a single voice performing characters. The [autonomy protocol](05-autonomy.md) says identity exists because each person writes their own material; authorship is the rule that protects that boundary on every file in the library.
