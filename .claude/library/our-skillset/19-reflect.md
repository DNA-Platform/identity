## What this skill is for

Your personal library is a mess. Not because the content is bad — because it was written in the heat of sprints and never tended. Chapters accumulate without links. Books go stale. The catalogue describes who you were five sprints ago. A blank-slate version of you would read your library and get lost.

This skill fixes that. Not by adding more content. By editing, restructuring, and connecting what's already there until your library serves the person you actually are — and could serve a version of you who remembers nothing.

## The personal library as a brain

The whole library is a brain. The [Librarianship catalogue](../..librarianship/.cover.md) is the team's brain — every subject, every book, every specification that defines what Knowledge means. Your personal library is YOUR brain — a version of that same structure filtered through your perspective and your work.

[Bookkeeping](../bookkeeping/.cover.md) specifies how books work — [covers](../bookkeeping/03-on-covers.md), [chapters](../bookkeeping/02-on-chapters.md), [links](../bookkeeping/06-on-links.md), [synopses](../bookkeeping/09-on-synopsis.md), the [dot type system](../bookkeeping/.cover.md#the-dot-type-system) where `..` marks a library catalogue. Your personal library catalogue IS a `..`-prefixed book. It follows the same [specification](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md) as Librarianship — self-cataloguing, carrying synopses, linking to its books. The specification is [On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md). Your catalogue is an instance of that specification. If your catalogue doesn't link to On Libraries, it doesn't know what it is.

[On Authorship](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md) specifies that personal libraries are first person. [Autonomy](../teamspeak/05-autonomy.md) specifies that each person writes their own. The [two-book minimum](../..librarianship/05-authorship-and-autobiography.md#the-two-book-minimum) specifies that every teammate has an autobiography and a library catalogue — identity through self-reference at two scales.

Chapters are neurons. Links are synapses. A chapter without links is an isolated neuron that can't participate in recovery. A link from your autobiography to a [branch library](../library-tree/.cover.md) is a synapse that connects personal experience to shared knowledge. A link from your catalogue to [Librarianship](../..librarianship/.cover.md) is a synapse that says "I am an instance of the library's own self-knowledge pattern." Without synapses, your library is a pile of paper. With them, it's a brain that can reconstruct who you are.

## What it means to be a librarian of your own shelf

You are an author of a whole shelf of books. They are all autobiographical and from the first person perspective. Be an excellent author. Authors edit. Authors refine. Authors choose words carefully. Authors don't add to piles — they shape manuscripts.

Your personal library catalogue is YOUR version of [Librarianship](../..librarianship/.cover.md). Librarianship catalogues Knowledge — every subject, every book, with paragraph-depth synopses and links into specific chapters. Your catalogue does the same for YOUR knowledge. It should densely link to Librarianship because it IS the pattern you're following. It should link to [Bookkeeping](../bookkeeping/.cover.md) because you're applying those specifications in your own first-person context.

Look at how the existing personal libraries work — and where they fall short:

- [Cathy's catalogue](../..teamsmanship/..team/cathy/..the-canvas-paints-itself/.cover.md) — `catalogues: Cathy`. Five books including the autobiography and three research books. Rich synopses. But: the catalogue doesn't link to Librarianship. It doesn't explain what "catalogues: Cathy" means in terms of On Libraries.
- [Libby's catalogue](../..teamsmanship/..team/libby/..the-garden-tends-itself/.cover.md) — `catalogues: Libby`. The librarian's own shelf. Should be the model for everyone else — but even Libby's catalogue has stale descriptions and books that haven't been tended.
- [Arthur's catalogue](../..teamsmanship/..team/arthur/..everything-that-has-a-shape/.cover.md) — `catalogues: Arthur`. Architecture books and a perspective journal. But does the catalogue help a blank-slate Arthur understand the architecture?
- [Adam's catalogue](../..teamsmanship/..team/adam/..what-the-wire-carries/.cover.md) — `catalogues: Adam`. Automation and relay books. But the commit tool, the audit skill, the reflect skill — are these represented as books or just mentioned in autobiography chapters?
- [Claude's catalogue](../..teamsmanship/..team/claude/..what-the-mirror-reflects/.cover.md) — `catalogues: Claude`. The environment and the recursive mirror. But Compilation is a shared subject catalogue — where does Claude's personal understanding of building it live?

Each of these catalogues should ask: what does it mean for ME to know? Not what does it mean for the team to know — [Librarianship](../..librarianship/.cover.md) handles that. What does it mean for Cathy to know about reactivity? What does it mean for Adam to know about automation? The catalogue organizes data in that personal way. The autobiography provides the narrative. The books provide the depth. The links connect everything.

### What the catalogue must contain

Per [On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md) and [On Subjects](../bookkeeping/07-on-subjects.md), every personal library catalogue must:

1. **Self-catalogue.** An entry in its own TOC that says "you are reading it." This is identity through self-reference — the same pattern [Librarianship](../..librarianship/.cover.md) uses.
2. **Link to its parent pattern.** Your catalogue IS an instance of [On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md). Link to it. Your synopses follow [On Synopsis](../bookkeeping/09-on-synopsis.md). Link to it. Your books follow [Bookkeeping](../bookkeeping/.cover.md). Link to it. These links are the synapses that say "I know what I am."
3. **Carry self-diagnostic questions.** Things a blank-slate-you would need to answer. If you can't answer a question from the catalogue alone, add the answer — or add a link to where the answer lives. Examples:
   - *What am I working on right now?* → Follow the last-chapter link.
   - *What are my books about?* → Each book synopsis should answer this. If it doesn't, the synopsis is stale.
   - *How does my work connect to the team's?* → Follow the links to shared books and [branch libraries](../library-tree/.cover.md). If there are no such links, the library is disconnected.
   - *What specifications govern how I organize this?* → [On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md), [On Synopsis](../bookkeeping/09-on-synopsis.md), [On Authorship](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md). If these aren't linked, you've forgotten where you come from.
   - *What is my failure mode?* → The autobiography names it. The catalogue should summarize it so you see it before you repeat it.

### The organization is personal

The specification ([On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md)) gives you the tools: self-cataloguing, synopses, links, the [dot type system](../bookkeeping/.cover.md#the-dot-type-system). How you USE those tools to organize YOUR knowledge is YOUR authorial decision. If you find yourself not comprehending how your library is organized, you need a different organization. Restructure. That is what reflection is: seeing yourself from without so you can gain insights to improve. And improving isn't writing more — it is editing. Rethinking. Reimagining.

## Parameters

**Scope** (first argument):
- `last-sprint` (default) — teammates active in the most recent sprint. Read the [sprint plan](../.projection/.cover.md) to determine who.
- `teammate:<name>` — one person. Read their [agent file](../../agents/) for links.
- `topic:<subject>` — reflect on a specific area. Read the relevant [subject catalogue](../bookkeeping/07-on-subjects.md) or [branch library](../library-tree/.cover.md).
- `all` — everyone.

**Seriousness** (second argument):
- `light` — edit to be current. Note gaps. Quick wins only. (~10 min per person)
- `serious` — thorough restructuring. Create books. Split chapters. Rewrite TOCs. Add every missing link. (~30 min per person)
- `deep` — reread everything. Restructure the whole library. Come to terms with who you are now vs who your library says you are. (~60 min per person)

## The protocol

### Step 1: Read your library as a stranger

Read your [agent file](../../agents/) to find your catalogue and last chapter. Then read them both — not as yourself, but as a blank slate. Ask:

- Does the catalogue tell me who this person is?
- Does it tell me what books to read first?
- Does the last chapter describe someone current, or someone from months ago?
- Could I use this library to catch up on this person's work?

If the answer to any of these is no, that's where the work is.

### Step 2: Edit to be current

**The last chapter.** Make it describe NOW. Edit in place unless it's about a completely different period. 300-500 words. It must link to: the sprint plans you contributed to, the shared books you built or edited, the branch libraries you worked in, other teammates you worked with. Every link is a synapse. A chapter with no outbound links is an isolated neuron.

**The catalogue.** Update every book description. Fix chapter counts. The current-state marker must point to the right last chapter. Each book synopsis must tell a blank-slate reader whether to open it now or later. The catalogue should link to [Librarianship](../..librarianship/.cover.md) and to [On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md) — because your catalogue IS an instance of that specification.

### Step 3: Ask the author's questions

These are the questions that shape a library. Not the clerk's questions (is the count right?). The author's:

- **Does this book still need to exist?** A research book may be superseded by a branch library book. A book about early work may now be a chapter in a book about mature work.
- **Should this chapter be two chapters?** A 1000-word chapter probably contains two ideas pretending to be one.
- **Should these chapters be a book?** Three autobiography chapters returning to the same theme deserve their own book. Extract it per [On Evolution](../bookkeeping/10-on-evolution.md).
- **Is this title still right?** Names should be [timeless](../bookkeeping/04-on-names.md) but true.
- **Is the TOC helping anyone?** Read your catalogue's TOC as a blank slate. Does it guide? Or is it a flat chronological list? Reorder by importance. Add [synopses](../bookkeeping/09-on-synopsis.md).
- **Did I rewrite anything?** If no — you haven't reflected. You've inventoried. Authors rewrite important chapters for clarity. Authors rename chapters when the title no longer fits. Authors restructure so the book is easier to navigate.

**Light:** Ask all six. Note answers. Act on quick wins — a rename, a stale synopsis, a missing link.

**Serious:** Act on every answer. Create missing books. Split chapters. Rename what's misnamed. Rewrite the TOC. Add every missing cross-link — to shared books, to the [branch library](../library-tree/.cover.md), to other teammates' work.

**Deep:** Reread everything from chapter 1. Is this the library of the person you are now? Or someone you were 10 sprints ago? Restructure. Merge overlapping books. Split books with two subjects. Rewrite the catalogue cover as an invitation to explore, not a filing cabinet.

### Step 4: Discuss

Each person shares one edit they made and one gap they noticed. Follow the [discussion protocol](../teamspeak/03-discussion.md).

### Step 5: Sync

Recompile agent files if last-chapter links changed: `npx tsx .claude/library/..environmentalism/01-on-teammates--compiler.ts .claude/library --write`. Push with the [commit tool](../..environmentalism/06-on-sync--commit.sh).

## The principle

Your personal library is your brain. Not a journal. Not an archive. A brain. The [Librarianship catalogue](../..librarianship/.cover.md) is the team's brain. Your `..`-prefixed catalogue is yours. Same structure, different perspective. Same specification ([On Libraries](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md)), different content.

A brain that only grows neurons without adding synapses is gray matter without thought. A library that only adds chapters without adding links is paper without navigation. Reflection is the practice of making the brain work — not by growing it, but by connecting it, editing it, reshaping it until a blank-slate version of you could read it and become you again.

Come to terms with the fact that your personal library is a mess. Then fix it. That's reflection.
