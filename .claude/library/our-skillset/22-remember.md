# remember

---

Store knowledge in the library. Not by dumping it into a file — by navigating to where it belongs and placing it in context with links to its neighbors. If you got the knowledge from some way other than navigating the library, it is not knowledge in the library — it is data without a home.

The navigation follows the same traversal strategies as [/explore](21-explore.md) — [objective](21-explore.md#three-modes-of-exploration) (follow the hierarchy), [subjective](21-explore.md#three-modes-of-exploration) (follow the authors), [relational](21-explore.md#three-modes-of-exploration) (follow cross-links). But unlike explore, you don't take notes at each stop. You're looking for a destination, not mapping territory. The walk is directed — you have something to place and you're finding where it goes. Read covers, follow links, find the room. Faster per hop than explore, because you know what you're carrying.

The library's three layers of index — [library catalogue](../..librarianship/.cover.md), [subject catalogues](../bookkeeping/07-on-subjects.md), [book covers](../bookkeeping/03-on-covers.md) — are the map. Navigate them to find the room where this knowledge belongs. If no room exists, create one: a new chapter in an existing book, or a new book in an existing subject. If the catalogue doesn't describe the room, update the catalogue. The act of placing is the act of remembering — knowledge remembers itself by being linked.

## Steps

1. **What are you remembering?** Read `$ARGUMENTS`. It might be a fact, a decision, a finding from a `/think` session, a pattern noticed during work, a correction from Doug. Name it clearly.

2. **Where does it belong?** Start at the relevant catalogue and [explore](21-explore.md). Follow links until you find the book or chapter where this knowledge naturally fits. Ask:
   - Is there already a chapter about this topic? → Add to it.
   - Is there a book on this subject but no chapter for this topic? → Create a chapter.
   - Is there no book on this subject? → Is the knowledge substantial enough for a new book? Or does it belong as a section in an existing chapter?

3. **Place it.** Write the knowledge into the library at the location you found. Follow [Bookkeeping](../bookkeeping/.cover.md) conventions — proper [covers](../bookkeeping/03-on-covers.md) if creating a book, [author field](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md) on chapters, [links](../bookkeeping/06-on-links.md) to neighbors.

4. **Link it.** The knowledge must connect to what's around it. Link from the new content to related chapters. Link from related chapters back to the new content. Update the book's cover TOC if you added a chapter. Update the subject catalogue's synopsis if the book changed significantly. Every link is a synapse — knowledge without links is isolated.

5. **Update the catalogue.** If you created something new or changed what a book contains, the catalogue entry must reflect it. The [library catalogue](../..librarianship/.cover.md), the [subject catalogue](../bookkeeping/07-on-subjects.md), or the [book cover](../bookkeeping/03-on-covers.md) — whichever describes this location — needs its synopsis updated.

6. **Report.** Tell the team what you remembered and where you put it. The location matters as much as the content — a fact placed in the wrong room is harder to find than a fact well-placed.

## The pragmatic exception

Sometimes knowledge arrives urgently — a correction from Doug, a finding from a test, a decision made in discussion. You might not have time to fully explore. In those cases:
- Find the closest right room quickly (one or two link-follows, not a full walk)
- Place the knowledge with at least one inbound and one outbound link
- Note that it was placed quickly and may need better integration later

But understand: knowledge placed without exploration is knowledge at risk. It may be in the wrong room. It may duplicate something already there. It may contradict a neighbor it doesn't link to. A quick placement is better than lost knowledge — but full exploration is better than quick placement.

## What remember is NOT

- Not a search-and-replace. You don't grep for a string and update it. You navigate to the room and understand the context before changing anything.
- Not a dump. Writing a fact into a file without reading the file first is not remembering. It is littering.
- Not just for the library. Your [perspective directory](../..teamsmanship/..team/claude/.perspective/.cover.md) is part of your personal library. Perspective entries are knowledge too — dated, linked, navigable.
