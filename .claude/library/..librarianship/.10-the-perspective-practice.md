# The perspective practice

Libby: Perspective is not a screenshot dump. It's an active practice with four steps: **represent → look → reflect → change.**

## The four steps

**Represent:** Create an artifact that makes something visible. A screenshot of the app. A diagram of the architecture. A coverage map of the tests. A rendering of the library's link structure. The artifact goes in a perspective chapter.

**Look:** Read the artifact. What do you see? What's visible that wasn't before? What patterns emerge? The looking is literal — read the image, study the diagram, examine the map.

**Reflect:** Write about what you saw. Not a caption — a reflection. What does this representation teach you? What would you change? What did you expect to see and didn't? The reflection is a chapter in the perspective book.

**Change:** Act on the reflection. Fix the bug the screenshot revealed. Refactor the architecture the diagram exposed. Fill the gap the coverage map showed. The change is the purpose — the perspective practice produces better work, not just better documentation.

## What each agent represents

Libby: The perspective practice is different for each agent because each agent SEES different things:

- **Arthur** represents architecture — dependency trees, system diagrams, the three-layer model. His perspective reveals structural patterns.
- **Cathy** represents the framework finding its form — Lab screenshots across sprints, reactive model diagrams. Her perspective reveals how $Chemistry evolves.
- **Libby** represents the library's structure — link maps, subject trees, reading cost measurements. Her perspective reveals how knowledge is organized.
- **Adam** represents infrastructure — message flow diagrams, relay paths, automation sequences. His perspective reveals where wires are thin.
- **David** represents the deployed state — GitHub Pages, package registry, deployment dashboards. His perspective reveals the world's view.
- **Phillip** represents the user experience — the Lab from the user's point of view, navigation flows, visual hierarchy. His perspective reveals whether the app teaches.
- **Queenie** represents the specification — coverage maps, promise clusters, gap analysis. Her perspective reveals what the tests say as a document.
- **Gabby** represents visual design — before/after comparisons, color studies, typography specimens. Her perspective IS the design journal.

## The perspective book

Libby: Each agent's library catalogues a perspective book. The perspective book contains:

- **Chapters** with prose reflections on what was represented and what it taught
- **Resources** — the artifacts themselves (images, diagrams, DOM dumps, etc.) stored alongside their chapter

Libby: The `.cover.md` of the perspective book describes what perspective means for this specific agent and lists the chapters. It's not generic — it's the agent's statement about how they see.

## When to use perspective

Libby: Use the perspective practice when:
- You're about to make a change and want to see the current state first
- You've made a change and want to verify it worked
- You need to understand something complex by representing it visually
- You're reviewing your own work and want an external view
- Doug asks "can you show me what you see?"

Libby: The perspective is the agent's way of making the invisible visible. The library records what was learned. The perspective records what was SEEN.
