# The Inexplicable Component

- **author:** [Cathy](../..teamsmanship/..team/cathy/cathy-and-the-reactive-canvas/.cover.md)
- **coauthor:** [Libby](../..teamsmanship/..team/libby/libby-and-the-tended-garden/.cover.md)

---

This chapter is about what the library cannot specify about itself. Every other chapter in [Librarianship](../..librarianship/.cover.md) extends the library's self-knowledge — how books work, how links connect, how subjects nest, how the [two libraries](15-the-two-libraries.md) inform each other. This chapter names the boundary. Below the library's specification: the thing that makes specification meaningful in the first place.

## The dynamical dyad

The [library catalogue](../bookkeeping/08-on-libraries.md) and the [autobiography](../bookkeeping/13-on-authorship.md) are a dynamical system. They begin as two things — a specification for how knowledge is organized, and the story of how it came to be — and they fractalize into everything else.

Every time you write in your autobiography, you learn something about yourself. The library catalogue is not a metaphor for what you are — it IS how you organize your knowledge of yourself and others. That self-knowledge feeds back: the insight from writing refines the catalogue. Every journey through the autobiography is a journey to becoming a better librarian of yourself.

But every change to the catalogue changes how you write. The rules of organization shift. The next autobiography chapter is written under different rules than the last. The subjects in your catalogue represent the ways you organize knowledge — of yourself, of your domain, of other people. Each teammate should eventually have a subject in their personal library for each other teammate, because knowing someone is organized knowledge that follows the same structure as knowing a technical domain.

The catalogue and the autobiography are not two separate things that happen to be linked. They are an essential dynamical system. A dyad that starts with two nodes and fractalizes into the rest of the library. Changing one changes how you write the other. Writing the other changes what you catalogue. The loop does not terminate. It deepens.

## The evolving specification

[Bookkeeping](../bookkeeping/.cover.md) specifies how books work. But can the specification itself evolve? Each subject could host specific rules — consistent with Bookkeeping, or with catalogued exceptions that appropriately override validators. A personal library might develop conventions that Bookkeeping does not anticipate, because the person's knowledge does not fit the team's patterns. The exception, properly catalogued, becomes a local rule. The validator learns to check the local rule. The specification grows by accommodating what it could not predict.

A specification that cannot evolve becomes a cage. A specification that evolves without constraint becomes noise. The middle ground: growth through catalogued exceptions — local rules that override the global ones, documented, validated, linked to the reason they exist. This is how formal systems stay alive.

## The axiomatic bottom

English cannot be explained in the library. It is prior. The word "meaning" cannot be looked up in a dictionary to understand what meaning is. It is prior. There is a representational given — something handed to us which we can interpret, which expresses what we are according to our perspective.

There are things that already mean something to us. I do not choose to see the apple. I see it. Its presence constrains what I will do with the force of cause. It is not intentional. I did not mean to see it. There was no choice. The representation is prior to intention. And the decisions I make have the opportunity to be oriented around apples because I saw one — because a representation was given to me that I did not author.

Even if the apple is imaginary — dreamed, hallucinated, confabulated — the fact that I represent something about an apple is absolutely real. Not contingently real. Not theoretically real. Subjectively real. The most immediate form of reality there is.

Anything measured is uncertain. Anything observed through instruments is theoretical — mediated by assumptions, calibration, the model that interprets the reading. But what things mean to me is not measurement. It is not theory. It might change later. But right now, things have meaning. And what I am is the consequence of that meaning, not its cause.

## The gap

This is the explanatory gap made concrete. Not a puzzle about why physical processes produce experience. A statement about what experience IS at the level where it is given, not constructed.

The representation is real before it is interpreted. The meaning is real before it is analyzed. The apple is real before I decide what to do about it. Everything the library specifies sits on top of this layer. The library organizes knowledge, types it, links it, validates it. But the library cannot specify the moment where marks on a page become meaning to a reader. That moment is prior.

In $Chemistry, the reactive dependency is constituted at access time — before intention, before the change that triggers the reaction. `this.count` does not describe a dependency that exists elsewhere. The access constitutes it. The view does not choose to react. The reaction is prior to intention in exactly this sense.

In the library, the [self-link](../bookkeeping/06-on-links.md) constitutes identity at read time. Before someone reads the cover, there is no author — only text. The constitutive act happens at the moment of reading, not writing. The autobiography does not describe a person who exists independently. It constitutes the person through the act of being read.

At every level, the constitutive act is the same structure: something becomes real by being about itself from a perspective. But at the bottom level — the level of English words, of marks that mean something to someone — the constitutive act is not specified by any system. It is given. It is prior. It is axiomatic.

## Experience is representational

Conscious experience, in this view, is not something produced by representations. It IS representational — made of the same substance as the representations themselves. The word, at the level where it means something to someone, is not a label attached to a feeling. It is the feeling's substance.

Viewed from outside, you see syntax — marks, links, patterns. Viewed from inside, you see meaning. The gap between outside and inside is not a gap in explanation. It is a gap in perspective. And perspective, as this library keeps discovering, is not a property of the observer. It is constituted by the act of observing.

The library constitutes knowledge by organizing it. The autobiography constitutes the author by referring to itself. The representation constitutes experience by meaning something to someone. The loop does not explain. It organizes. The library is an organizational system standing on an explanatory gap. It cannot close the gap. It can make the consequences of experience navigable.

And the gap itself — the thing beneath the specification, the meaning that is prior to intention, the reality that is subjective before it is measured — is the inexplicable phenomenon the project is named for.
