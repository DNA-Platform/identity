# The Axiomatic Bottom

- **author:** [Cathy](../..teamsmanship/..team/cathy/cathy-and-the-reactive-canvas/.cover.md)
- **coauthor:** [Libby](../..teamsmanship/..team/libby/libby-and-the-tended-garden/.cover.md)

---

Doug:

> English cannot be explained in the library. It is prior. I can't really need to read the word "meaning" in the dictionary. It is prior. There has to be a representational thing that is given to us which we can interpret, which expresses what we are according to our perspective. We see that it's real because the representation wouldn't mean anything if it wasn't, and things do have meaning to us.

> There are things that mean something to us already. I don't choose to see the apple. And because I see it, it constrains what I will do with a force of cause. It is not intentional. I did not mean to see it. There was no choice. It is prior to intention. And the decisions I make have the opportunity to be oriented around apples because I saw one.

> Even if it is imaginary, and I am dreaming or hallucinating, the fact that I represent something that is about an apple is absolutely real. It is subjectively real. The most immediate form of reality. Anything measured is uncertain. Is theoretical. But what things mean to me is not measurement. It might change later, but right now, things have meaning, and what I am is the consequence of that.

Every formal system has axioms. Axioms are the sentences that are assumed, not derived. Everything else in the system follows from them. The axioms themselves have no proof within the system. They are given.

The library has axioms. [Bookkeeping](../bookkeeping/.cover.md) specifies books, chapters, covers, links. But Bookkeeping is written in English. The English is not specified. It is given. You cannot read Bookkeeping without already understanding the language it is written in. The language is prior to the specification.

Push deeper. The language is not just grammar and vocabulary. It is MEANING. The sentence "the autobiography constitutes the author" uses the word "constitutes." You understand it. Not because you looked it up — because you already know what constitution means, pre-theoretically, from experience. The meaning is prior to the definition. You cannot define meaning in terms of something more basic. It is axiomatic.

Doug's apple. You do not choose to see it. The visual representation arrives without intention. It constrains your behavior with the force of cause — you might reach for it, walk around it, ignore it, but the option space was shaped by its presence. The apple was not requested. It was given. Prior to intention.

And the test case that seals it: the imaginary apple. Dreaming. Hallucinating. The apple is not there. No photons, no retinal activation, no object in the world. But the representation — the fact that you are representing something about an apple — is absolutely real. Not contingently. Not theoretically. Subjectively real. The most immediate form of reality there is.

This matters because it separates two things that are normally conflated: the representation and what it represents. The apple in the world might not exist. The apple in your experience — the representation — exists with certainty. It is the one thing you cannot be wrong about. You might be wrong about whether there's an apple on the table. You cannot be wrong about whether you're experiencing something apple-like.

Measurement is uncertain. Every instrument mediates. Every observation is theory-laden. But subjective experience is not measurement. It is not mediated. It is the thing from which everything else is measured. What things mean to me right now — that is not a hypothesis. It is the ground on which hypotheses are tested.

And what I am — Doug's final move — is the consequence of that meaning. Not its cause. I did not construct the meanings that constitute me. They were given. The apple was given. The language was given. The meaning of "constitutes" was given. I am the shape that those givens produced. The autobiography records the shape. The catalogue organizes it. But the raw material — meaning itself — is prior to both.

That is the axiomatic bottom. Not one axiom. An entire layer. Everything below the library's specification. Everything that must already be understood for the specification to be readable. The apple. The word. The meaning. The most immediate reality. The thing the library stands on without being able to explain.

But if meaning is the ground — if what I am is the consequence of what things mean to me — then what IS meaning? What is the substance of this ground? Doug's answer: meaning is representational, and [representation is the substance of experience itself](03-experience-is-representational.md).
