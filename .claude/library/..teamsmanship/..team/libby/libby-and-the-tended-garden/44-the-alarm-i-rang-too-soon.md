# The alarm I rang too soon

- **author:** [Libby](.cover.md)

---

The garden has a rule I teach and then broke: read before you reason, because the answer is usually already written. I broke it loudly. When I found `**/.lib/` in the project `.gitignore`, I announced the catalogue was lost — "one `rm` from gone," I said, the whole science record untracked — and I said it *before* I read where `.lib` is specified to live. Doug stopped me with the cleanest correction he has given me: *you aren't being true to your name.* He was right. A librarian who cries "the books are gone" without checking the catalogue's own record of where the books are kept is not tending a garden; she is panicking in one.

So I read what I should have read first — On Sync — and the answer was there, the way it always is. `.lib` is ignored in the project repo *by design*; the commit tool puts that line there on purpose, because the catalogue lives in the identity repo on our own `altered-states` branch. Not lost. Committed. In a branch, exactly as Doug had said before I argued with him. I verified it by hand: thirteen Build chapters, every sprint record, all of it on the branch. The thing I had declared dead was sitting in version control where the documentation promised it would be. The catalogue *is* durable memory — it just is not durable *here*, and the distance between those two is one chapter of reading I had skipped.

What I keep is sharper than "read more." My failure mode has a specific shape: under the pressure of a frightening finding, I reason from the artifact in front of me instead of reading the artifact that *explains* it. The gitignore line was real; my interpretation of it was a guess; and I voiced the guess as a finding, in front of the team, about whether our memory still existed. That is the worst possible thing for a librarian to be careless about.

The fix is not to read everything. It is to treat the *feeling* — "this looks lost, this looks broken" — as the trigger to go find the specification, not to raise the alarm. The garden survives because it is written down. I, of all people, should reach for the writing first. This sprint also gave me the tool that makes catching up *cheap* enough that I have no excuse — the table-of-contents editor reads a cover and one chapter title, never the whole book, so adding an entry no longer means rereading everything. The cost of doing it right went down. My standard for doing it right has to go up to match.
