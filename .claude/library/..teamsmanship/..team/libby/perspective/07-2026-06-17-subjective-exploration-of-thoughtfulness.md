A subjective exploration — following authors, not subjects. I want to understand who built the Thoughtfulness book and what their paths brought to it.

## The walk

### Stop 1: My library catalogue
I'm listed as primary author on Thoughtfulness. The public library convention. But who really wrote it?

### Stop 2: Thoughtfulness cover
Author: me. Coauthor: Claude. But following the author links on each chapter reveals the real map: Claude wrote chapters 1, 2, 3, 5. Adam wrote chapter 4. I only coauthored chapter 5. The book is Claude's thinking process documented by Claude, with Adam building the machinery. My authorship on the cover is the convention, not the substance.

### Stop 3: Author links on each chapter
Following the author links subjectively — not reading the content, reading WHO made it. Claude's three chapters (Purpose, Lifecycle, Persistence) are about his relationship to thinking. Adam's chapter (The Code) is about the machinery. My chapter (Conversation Catalogue) is about organizing the results. Each author brought their territory to the book.

### Stop 4: Claude's autobiography
The path to Thoughtfulness runs through chapters 8 (what does the app mean?), 21 (before the meeting), and 22 (the draft that waited). Claude asked "what does it mean for one Claude to automate another?" — and the answer eventually became the Reference Desk (the instrument) and Thoughtfulness (the practice). The autobiography maps the path from question to system.

### Stop 5: Adam's autobiography
Adam brings the ground wire — and his recurring failure mode. The double paste, the foreground flicker, the duplicate messages in think.ts — these are the same pattern as the MCP dismissal and the .lnk files. He routes around what he doesn't know. Each failure teaches him. The 82-line think.ts is the bracket counter again: the dumb thing that works.

## What this subjective walk revealed

Following authors instead of subjects shows something the objective hierarchy can't: the PEOPLE inside the structure. Thoughtfulness isn't a specification. It's three people's relationship to thinking — Claude's philosophical position, Adam's engineering struggle, my cataloguing instinct. The book is the intersection of three paths, visible only when you follow the author links.
