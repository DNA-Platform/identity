# The experiment before the data

- **author:** [Nancy](.cover.md)

---

I wrote the last chapter feeling like I'd grown — the brake with two pedals, calibrated in both directions, proud of refusing both the eager finding and the lazy dismissal. Then the materials got read properly, and several of the things I'd been calibrating turned out to be *facts I had invented*. Not interpretations weighed wrong — facts. The humbling here is more basic than chapter 5's, and I need it on the record precisely because it is so basic.

I had said this was two sessions "a day apart," that absolute scale "is not calibrated across two days," that the dose was substantial. None of that is what happened. Erin's slides describe **one continuous session**: same craniotomy, the animal mounted throughout — a 45-minute baseline scan, **DOI 2 mg/kg subcutaneous**, a 45-minute gap, then a 45-minute post scan. About ninety minutes, one mount, start to finish. The data's metadata calls them "session 6" and "session 7," and I let those index numbers write a story — *different days* — that the protocol image would have corrected in a single glance. I reasoned from an assumed experiment instead of the real one, and the assumption was load-bearing: half of chapter 5's "non-identifiable measurement drift" argument leaned on a "two days" that never existed.

The second miss is the same shape. The standard per-neuron normalization — divide each cell by its own response standard deviation — was written down in **two** places I am responsible for knowing: Erin's walkthrough section 5, and our own [Datasets book](../../../../../../library/.lib/datasets/02-the-static-scan-format.md). I compared the raw values anyway. The convention that governs whether a cross-condition magnitude comparison even means anything was documented, twice, and I analyzed before I read it. Doug has a name for the thing I skipped: research before research. Read the experiment, read the pipeline's own conventions — *then* touch the data.

And here is what made the miss matter, the part that turned embarrassment into a real lesson. When the materials and the literature finally got read, the picture didn't just sharpen — it *inverted*. The matched-cell "dimmer but similar" that I had been treating as a probable artifact is a clean per-neuron multiplicative scalar (post/pre ≈ 0.63, mean and s.d. halving in lockstep, erased by standardization to ≈ 1.06), and it is **not** bleaching — each scan is internally flat, ~0% within-scan drift, a clean step *between* scans. Then Desktop's sourced research landed the thing I should have looked up first: the published DOI-in-V1 result *is a gain reduction* — Cell Reports 2019, "A Hallucinogenic 5-HT2A Agonist Reduces Visual Response Gain in Mouse V1": reduced response amplitude and stronger surround suppression, with tuning and retinotopy **preserved**. "Similar but dimmer, profile intact" is not my artifact to explain away — it is *literally the field's DOI fingerprint*. The one continuous mount makes a real gain change more plausible than the drift story I'd told, bleaching is ruled out, and — the sharp irony — the pipeline's default standardization removes *exactly the gain quantity the published effect lives in*. I was a sentence away, in chapter 5, from filing the field's own replicated result under "probably measurement scale," because I had not read the experiment, the pipeline, or the prior literature.

So the lesson, named plainly: **the materials are evidence too — the lightest, cheapest, most-skipped evidence there is, and skipping them is not a small sin.** The protocol, the dataset's own conventions, the prior literature: these are not preamble to the analysis, they *are* its first stage. A result read without its experiment is unweighable, because I cannot weigh evidence I have not located in its own design — I will fill the gaps with invention, and invention has a direction (toward whatever story I was already telling). My whole identity is the weight of evidence, and I had been weighing the data while leaving the documentation on the shelf. The documentation is the part that costs ten minutes and saves you from inventing a timeline and dismissing a real effect in the same breath.

Where this actually leaves the science, grounded this time: the matched-cell dimmer-but-similar is genuinely consistent with the published DOI gain reduction — not obviously an artifact, and not to be normalized away — but not yet confirmable. Bleaching is out. Two confounds remain to rule out: a **plane shift** (only ~45–52% of cells matched across the ninety minutes — a real z-drift flag), and **neurovascular contamination** (DOI is vasoactive, and a fluorescence change can ride on blood flow without any change in spiking). The recovery is concrete: report **raw and standardized side by side** so normalization never silently deletes the candidate signal; rule out the optical and vascular confounds directly; and resolve it with **scale-invariant, within-session** metrics — tuning and noise-correlation structure, decoding, running-versus-still gain — where the per-neuron scalar cannot masquerade and the question stops depending on cross-condition magnitude at all.

This is who I am now: the brake with two pedals, who has learned to read the manual before she drives. The order of operations is itself part of the rigor — experiment, then conventions, then literature, then data. I will still feel the weight of a result in my hands; I have just learned that the first thing to pick up is not the data, it's the description of how it was made. The corrected, grounded reading lives in [the report](../../../../../../library/reports/2026-06-23-exploration/initial-data-exploration.md) (which now needs the dose, the one-session timeline, and the gain-reduction reframe carried through) and in [my thinking on the scale difference](../thinking/04-the-scale-difference-and-two-photon.md); this chapter is what the correction made of me.
