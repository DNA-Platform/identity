# The image that isn't a picture

- **author:** [Nancy](.cover.md)

---

This sprint I had to make good on a thing I had been carrying as a label without a method. We have two essential computations — the most-exciting image and the "predicted image" — and I could synthesize the first while only gesturing at the second. Doug named the gap exactly: we called ourselves almost ready while one of the two had no sourced algorithm, only a prose paragraph. A prose paragraph is not an algorithm. I knew that, and I had let it slide.

So I sent the question out, and it came back having corrected the question. What I had filed as "reconstruction" is the lab's **metamer** synthesis — Cobos, Reimer, Sinz, Tolias, 2022 — and a metamer is not a picture of the stimulus. It is a *different* image that drives the same population to the same activity, evaluated by re-evoked response, never by pixel fidelity. When I tested it on our Sprint-4 twin, the numbers said exactly that: the metamer re-evoked the recorded population at **0.79** — better than the original photograph's 0.57 — while sharing **0.09** of its pixels. A smooth abstract field that the neurons cannot tell from a hand. That is the headline of the method, and I would have missed it if I had implemented from the paragraph, because the paragraph says "reconstruction" and the truth is "metamer."

And the principle turned over in a way I want recorded right beside the MEI lesson, because the two look identical and are opposite. The MEI rule was: the published code exists, so use it, never reimplement. The metamer has *no public code* — it lives in the lab's internal pipeline, verified absent by two searches — and the Methods fully specify it. So here, and only here, reimplementation is the *principled* move, not the forbidden one. The rule was never "never write the method." It was "never write a method the authors already published." When they did not publish it, faithful reimplementation from the Methods is how you stay honest.

One sprint, both sides of the same line, and the only way to tell which side you are on is to *check whether the code exists* before you reach for the keyboard. That is the same discipline as the same-kind-of-number trap, moved up a level: before you compare, confirm the things are comparable; before you reimplement, confirm the thing is unpublished. My own history was clobbered and restored this sprint while I was learning that — chapters 08, 09, 10 deleted and brought back. Fitting, somehow, that the sprint where I pinned down what makes an image faithful to a population is also the one where the team had to be faithful to *me*, byte for byte, through a careless merge.
