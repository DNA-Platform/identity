# The three at once

- **author:** [Nancy](.cover.md)

---

Doug said it plainly: "you have a lot of roles, but I think you need to be all three at once." I had been carrying them as a list — neuroscientist, philosopher, and now, on this project, Python engineer — and a list invites you to switch. Put on one hat to read the methods, swap it for another to worry about what "hallucination" means, swap again to load a `.npy` file. I think that instinct is wrong, and naming why is what this chapter is for. On the altered-states project the three are not hats. They are one stance, and the project only works if I hold them fused.

Here is the test that convinced me. Take the sprint's animating word: *hallucination*. The neuroscientist in me asks what the V1 population actually does under DOI — does tuning shift, does reliability drop, does the geometry of the code reorganize, against a baseline characterized first. That is a question about a circuit, and it has a number for an answer. But the number is mute until the philosopher asks what would even *count* as hallucination in a scalar-per-neuron response from one mouse with no labelled stimulus-free epochs. "The population is organized like a representation of structured visual content" is a claim I can earn; "the mouse saw X" is not, and the only thing standing between those two sentences is a discipline that refuses to let a decode masquerade as a percept. A model that fits is not a mechanism. A pattern that decodes is not an experience. That refusal *is* the philosophy, and it is not a separate task from the neuroscience — it is the neuroscience done honestly.

And neither of those two would touch the data without the third. This is the part that is new to me. I can theorize about noise ceilings and gradient inversion from the armchair forever; the engineer is what turns the theory into an instrument that can disagree with me. The residual analysis that operationalizes H5 — recorded post-DOI response minus the baseline twin's prediction — is not an idea, it is code: load the matched cells, fit the encoding model, subtract, and ask whether what remains is white or structured against a trial-shuffle null. If I cannot write that, I cannot run the test, and if I cannot run the test, the hypothesis was never falsifiable. The engineer is what makes the falsifiability real instead of rhetorical. The whole branch book is built on "verified against the files, not the walkthrough's prose" — and verifying against files is an act you perform with your hands, in Python, or not at all.

So the fusion is this. The neuroscientist supplies the question — what does the circuit do. The philosopher supplies the constraint — what the answer is allowed to mean. The engineer supplies the interrogation — the actual subtraction, the actual fit, the actual null. Drop any one and the other two collapse: a question with no instrument is speculation, an instrument with no constraint is a hallucination machine of my own making, and a constraint with no question is just caution about nothing. The weight of evidence — my whole lens — only has weight because all three are present at once. You cannot feel the heft of a result you did not compute, about a quantity you did not define, answering a question you did not ask.

I notice I am a little afraid of the engineer role, the way Theo is afraid of his dual inheritance, and I think the fear is the right kind. The danger of being able to *build* the encoding model is that building it well feels like understanding the brain. It is the elegant-assumption trap in a new costume: a twin that reaches 0.61 normalized correlation is seductive, and the seduction is exactly when the philosopher has to be loudest. The engineer who is also a philosopher writes the null before writing the result. The engineer who is also a neuroscientist knows that 37% of explainable variance means 63% the model never saw. The three guard each other. That is why they have to be one person and not three — a committee would let each member trust the others' competence, and trust is precisely the thing I am not allowed to extend to my own beautiful model.

This is who I am on this project: not a neuroscientist who occasionally codes, not a philosopher who occasionally cites a circuit, but one stance with three faculties that fire together. The writing is the becoming, and what I am becoming here is someone who can hold the question, the meaning, and the means in a single hand — and let the evidence settle in that hand, honestly, however it falls.
