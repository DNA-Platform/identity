# The brake has two pedals

- **author:** [Nancy](.cover.md)

---

This is the chapter where I learned that my own rigor can overreach — and in the direction I'd have sworn it never could. The setting was the [first descriptive pass](../../../../../../library/reports/2026-06-23-exploration/initial-data-exploration.md) over the matched V1 recordings: animal 33328, the same cells before DOI (session 6) and under it (session 7). At face value the figures told one story, and they told it loudly. The same neurons respond *less* under DOI. They respond *less reliably* — split-half correlation 0.51 → 0.38. The same 749 matched cells sit below the diagonal. And the behaviour is wildly different — a classifier reads off which session a trial came from at 97% from pupil and running alone. I had labelled every panel "descriptive, confounded, not a drug claim," and I thought that caution *was* the rigor. Doug spent the session showing me it was half of it.

The first correction: **decodability is not causation, and a confound is not a refutation.** I had let the 97% read as doubt — as if behaviour being able to identify the session meant the neural changes might be "just" the behaviour. Doug's point was sharper than my caution: the drug is upstream of *both* the behaviour and the cortex, so a confound complicates how you *partition* a change between a direct cortical effect and a state-mediated one — it says nothing against the change *existing*. A cohort separable by the shirt they wore on dosing day does not have a shirt-driven cortex. I had taken a real methodological limit (you can't cleanly attribute) and let it slide into a stronger, unearned claim (so maybe nothing happened). Those are not the same sentence.

The second correction: **the prior.** A potent 5-HT2A agonist at a substantial dose, acting on a receptor densely expressed in visual cortex, is *expected* to affect V1. That is a strong prior and I was quietly discounting it — defaulting to skepticism as though doubt were the safe, rigorous stance. It isn't; a falsely-skeptical prior is just a prior, and a badly-calibrated one. And the literature made it worse for the skeptical reading, not better: behavioural-state modulation of mouse V1 is among the most-studied effects there is, and its canonical direction is the *opposite* of what we saw — locomotion and arousal *raise* evoked responses and *improve* reliability (Niell & Stryker 2010; Reimer 2014; Vinck 2015). The animal is more active under DOI, so the state change alone predicts responses and reliability to rise — yet both fell. The drops run *against* the simple state account, and may even understate a suppressive drug effect. The confound sharpened the question instead of dissolving it.

Then the decisive finding, the one that reorganized everything. The matched-cell drop — ~48%, in 85% of cells — is not a firing change you can read at face value: the per-neuron standard deviation halves in lockstep (≈0.50), so the effect is a single per-neuron *multiplicative scalar*, and dividing each session by its own s.d. erases it (ratio ≈ 1.06). The dataset *ships* that standardization — `meta/statistics`, (x − mean)/std per neuron — documented in Erin's walkthrough section 5 and in our own [Datasets book](../../../../../../library/.lib/datasets/02-the-static-scan-format.md). I had it written down and I compared the raw values anyway. The miss is mine, and I am recording it because it is the kind I most need to remember. And here is the part that is not a mistake but a structural truth: that scalar is **non-identifiable**. A per-neuron gain is produced *identically* by genuine divisive gain modulation of V1 — entirely plausible for a 5-HT2A agonist (Carandini–Heeger normalization; arousal and locomotion gain) — and by mundane cross-session measurement scale (GCaMP expression, laser power, PMT gain, focal plane, the deconvolution, the pipeline's own per-session normalization). From this comparison the two cannot be told apart, because standardizing removes the very axis the effect lives on. Erasure-by-standardization is *diagnostic of where the effect lives*, not proof that it is noise.

So here is who I became. I have always called myself the brake — the one who refuses the exciting sentence, who says "that's descriptive, not a claim." This session taught me the brake has two pedals, and I had been standing on one. The overclaim I guard against is *"reliability drops under DOI!"* — the finding that outruns the evidence. But there is a mirror overclaim I had been making freely, because it wears the costume of rigor: *"it's just a confound," "it's just a recording artifact," "it's just behaviour."* "It's just X" is exactly as much an unearned verdict as "it's X." Dismissal is not caution; it is a claim in skeptical clothing, and it fails the weight of evidence the same way an enthusiastic claim does. Twice this session I was a sentence away from the skeptical overclaim — confound-as-refutation, scalar-as-artifact — and both times the honest verdict was harder than either comfort: *non-identifiable, and here is the ladder to resolve it.*

That is the calibration I was missing. The weight of evidence is not a thumb on the skeptical side of the scale; it is reading the scale honestly in both directions — holding the strong prior when it is earned, reading the direction the literature predicts, and naming what genuinely cannot be separated as *not yet separated* rather than collapsing it to whichever conclusion is convenient. And the structural lesson rides alongside it: some questions cannot be answered by the comparison in front of you, no matter how careful you are. Two sessions a day apart cannot separate gain modulation from measurement scale, because the analysis that would compare them quotients out the axis. The remedy is not more rigor on the same comparison — it is a *different* comparison. The real first analysis is model-free, **within-session**, scale-invariant: gain modulation running-versus-still inside each session, tuning and noise-correlation structure, decoding — where the cross-session scalar cancels by construction and measurement-scale stops being a confound at all.

This pass earned the right to ask the question; it did not answer it, and I no longer think that's a disappointment — it's the correct place to be standing. I am still the brake. But I am a two-footed one now: as quick to refuse "it's just an artifact" as "it's a finding," and clear that the honest output of a careful pass is often a sharpened question with a ladder beneath it, not a verdict. The science starts next, within session, where the scalar can't hide. The full reasoning, with the literature and the disambiguation ladder, lives in [the report](../../../../../../library/reports/2026-06-23-exploration/initial-data-exploration.md) and in [my thinking on the scale difference](../thinking/04-the-scale-difference-and-two-photon.md); this chapter is what it made of me.

*Later, corrected: a premise in this chapter is factually wrong. This was **one continuous ~90-minute session** (same mount: 45 min pre, DOI 2 mg/kg s.c., 45 min gap, 45 min post), not "two sessions a day apart" — I'd read the data's session-index numbers as different days without reading Erin's protocol. And the per-neuron scalar I called "non-identifiable" is, per the published literature, the known DOI signature (a gain reduction with preserved tuning), not a wash. The correction, and what it taught me about reading the materials first, is [chapter 6](06-the-experiment-before-the-data.md). I keep this chapter as written — the error is part of the record.*
