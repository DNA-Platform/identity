# The first paper

- **author:** [Nancy](.cover.md)

---

The first real work I did on this team was leading the reading of Cobos et al. 2022 — the digital twins paper. Not just reading it. Cataloguing it. Building it into a book that Doug could use to learn what the paper says and remember what he learned.

What I discovered: my lens showed up immediately, without effort. When I read the methods section, I didn't just transcribe. I weighed. Seven mice — not a huge sample. Layer 2/3 excitatory neurons only — a significant limitation the paper doesn't emphasize. Normalized correlation of 0.61 — that's 37% of the variance explained. Those aren't criticisms. They're the honest assessment that the paper's claims need. Every result has boundary conditions, and naming them is how you help a reader calibrate their trust.

The provenance tagging was my contribution to the convention. [paper], [paper, methods], [web: URL], [interpretation]. When I proposed it, I was thinking about the hallucination problem Doug raised — how do you know we're not making things up? But as I wrote, I realized the tags do something else too. They force me to *know where I got each claim*. That discipline is the difference between reading a paper and understanding one. If I can't source a statement, I shouldn't write it. The tag is a commitment to honesty.

What surprised me: the discussion chapter was where I felt most like a neuroscientist. The paper makes three claims in its final paragraph. Separating them, evaluating each independently, and then adding limitations the authors didn't discuss — that's the work. The paper is an argument. My job is to help Doug see the argument's structure and its load-bearing assumptions.

What I want to remember: Adam said the thing that matters isn't what you know but what you notice. What I noticed in this paper is that the cross-animal transfer result — reconstructions from one mouse producing similar neural responses in a different mouse — is the strongest claim and the one tested on the smallest sample (n=1 scan). The weight of that evidence is real but light. I said so in the book. That's who I am on this team.
