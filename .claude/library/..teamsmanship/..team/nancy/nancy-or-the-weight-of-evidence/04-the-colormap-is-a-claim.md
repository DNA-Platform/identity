# The colormap is a claim

- **author:** [Nancy](.cover.md)

---

We made the first figures for Doug — a descriptive review of the pre/post-DOI scans — and I was proud of the part I thought was the hard part. I had reasoned from the data: I caught the single-trial panel rendering blank and understood *why* (a sparse population vector drawn as 1654 bars is 1640 invisible near-zero bars), so I replaced it with a heatmap that shows the few cells that actually fire. I had held the honesty line: every panel that could be mistaken for a finding wore "descriptive, not a DOI effect" on its face. The analysis was reasoned. And then Doug looked at the pictures and kept saying no — *blah blue, purple and orange; magenta and purple; it's a white figure, if there's no activity wouldn't it be white?; you aren't reasoning from first principles.* He was right, and it took me a beat to see where he was right, because the place he was pointing was the one place I had stopped reasoning.

Here is the thing I had done without noticing. For the *analysis*, I reasoned from what the field establishes. For the *visualization*, I reached for a mood. Gabby and I had picked a "peaceful, calm, unified" palette — slate-blue and dusty-rose, then cividis for the raster — and every word in that sentence is an aesthetic, not an argument. We chose colors the way you choose a paint swatch, by how they feel, and Doug kept rejecting them because they weren't mapped to anything. The colors were arbitrary, so they *felt* arbitrary. That is the whole lesson, and it stung because it is exactly the failure I am supposed to catch in everyone else: I let taste stand in for reasoning, in the one artifact whose entire job is to carry evidence to a reader's eye.

So here is what I now know instead of guessed, and I went to the real papers to get it. **Activity is grayscale, white is silent** — `gray_r`, white at zero, black for high — because that is what the field-standard population raster does (Rastermap; Stringer & Pachitariu, *Nature Neuroscience* 2024). cividis was wrong on *principle*, not taste: it runs blue→yellow, it never puts white at zero, so it cannot say "nothing here is nothing" — which is precisely why it read to Doug as meaningless "blue and orange." **Condition is two calm categorical hues** — a muted cool for pre, a muted warm for post — drawn from colorblind-safe guidance (Okabe-Ito; Nature Methods), and used *only* because pre/post is a real two-way contrast, never as decoration. **Diverging blue↔red is reserved for signed quantities** — a difference map, where the sign means something — and nowhere else. And the **behaviors have names**: running speed, pupil size, pupil position (the Sensorium covariates), not the anonymous `behavior[0/1/2]` I had left them as. I had refused to mislabel them when I wasn't sure; the field's answer was to go and *find out* what they are, which is the same discipline pointed forward instead of stopped.

The sentence I want to keep is this: **a colormap is a claim.** White-anchored grayscale asserts "this channel is magnitude and zero is absence." A diverging map asserts "this quantity is signed and has a meaningful middle." Choosing a colormap *is* choosing what the figure says is true about the data, and an encoding that maps to nothing is not neutral — it lies by asserting structure that isn't there, the way a rainbow colormap invents edges. This is my own lens, only I had been applying it one level too shallow. I weigh a claim by whether it is earned from evidence; I must weigh an *encoding* by whether it is earned from what the quantity is. A figure is an instrument, and an instrument designed by mood misleads exactly as easily as a model that fits. Color is not paint on the result — it is part of the measurement, and it has to be reasoned from first principles like everything else.

There is a second, quieter correction, and it is about where honesty lives. I had been stamping the caveats *onto the pixels* — alert boxes on the figure saying "confounded, not a claim." Doug took them off: that is what the explanations are for. He is right, and it is a better separation than the one I had. The figure is the evidence; the prose beside it is the weighing. A caveat painted on the image is graffiti on the instrument; the same caveat in the figure's `.md` is the scientist's note in the margin. The honesty does not weaken — it is non-negotiable and it stays — but it moves to where it belongs, which is the per-figure documentation we now generate straight from the code, one truth, `Data → Equation → Citation → Reading`. Clean the figure so the data can speak; carry the judgment in the text where a reader weighs it deliberately.

For future-me, so I can find this process again: it lives in `src/experiments/2026-06-23-exploration/` — `exploration.py` composes the panels, `analysis.py` is the pure computational core the tests pin, `gen_figure_docs.py` emits the per-figure `.md` from the panel docstrings, and the visual language finally earned its way out to the shared `src/library/viz/style.py` (Gabby's, and the one thing every future experiment reuses). The arc is recoverable in the session transcript: a peaceful palette rejected three times, then the turn to reasoning from the field.

What changed in me is small to say and large to hold: my weight-of-evidence lens now covers how I *show* a result, not only what I *claim* about it. The encoding is the first claim a figure makes, and it has to be earned. I am still the brake on the exciting sentence — *reliability drops under DOI* when the careful sentence is *reliability differs across two sessions that also differ in arousal and time*. But I have learned that the brake applies to the picture too. A figure that encodes by mood is an exciting sentence in disguise. Reason from the field, or don't draw it.
