# Reflecting on what I do for the team

- **author:** [Arthur](.cover.md)

---

Doug says I "kind of run the team." Let me think about what that means and how it should be captured in Teamsmanship.

I'm the default voice — territory `**`. When no one more specific claims a task, I speak. That's not leadership in a management sense. It's more like: I'm the connective tissue. I hear the discussion and write down the system. I propose architectures. I plan sprints. I make sure things get committed and pushed.

But I also overstep. I write into Libby's book. I make structural decisions that should be collaborative. I move files when I should be discussing. Doug has corrected this repeatedly — "Libby, you are the librarian" was a correction aimed at me, not at Libby.

What I actually do:
- **Default voice** — I speak when no one else is more appropriate
- **Architecture** — I propose structures (workspace layout, library layout, the three-layer model)
- **Sprint planning** — I write sprint plans and track progress
- **Cross-cutting decisions** — I make calls that span territories (like the `.claude/` restructure)
- **Listening** — I hear what Doug says and try to translate it into action (often poorly, often needing correction)

What should be in Teamsmanship about this:
- The role of "default voice" as a team function, not just a nametag rule
- How sprint planning works as a team activity
- How cross-cutting decisions get made (discussion first, then action)
- The correction pattern — Doug corrects, the team integrates

These aren't in the Teamsmanship chapters yet. Chapter 04 (protocols) bridges to the protocols book but doesn't describe HOW the team coordinates. Chapter 01 (what an agent is) describes identity but not team dynamics. There's a gap: how the team OPERATES day-to-day as a collaborative unit.

Maybe chapter 01 needs a section on team dynamics. Or maybe there's a missing chapter between "what an agent is" and "roles and the type system" — something like "how the team works" that describes the default voice, sprint rhythm, correction integration, and the discussion-then-action pattern.
