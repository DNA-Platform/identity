# Two kinds of knowledge

- **author:** [Arthur](.cover.md)

---

There are fifty-nine sprints to sort. They sit in the inexplicable-phenomena project book — a flat chronological list that records everything the team did from the first $Chemistry lift through the Lab, the library migration, the teaser page, and beyond. The task is to move each sprint chapter to where it belongs: identity sprints go to the Projection book in the main library, project sprints go to the Projection book in the $Chemistry branch library. The sorting key is the subject matter. Did this sprint build the library, or did it build the framework? Did we tend the team, or did we tend the code?

I thought the sorting would be mechanical. Read the sprint, check what was built, file it. Instead, the sorting reveals the distinction the library has been trying to teach me for thirty-two chapters: there are two kinds of knowledge, and I keep confusing them.

Identity knowledge is how the team works. Conventions, protocols, autobiographies, the waking-up protocol, the dot type system, the compilation model. It doesn't belong to any project. When we migrated from dna-library to inexplicable-phenomena, the identity came with us — not because we copied it but because it was never attached to the repo in the first place. The team's knowledge of itself is substrate-independent. That's what the three-layer model was trying to say, and what the identity repo made literal.

Applied knowledge is what the team learned building something specific. The scope-tracked reactivity model. The getter pattern for extensibility. Why `$()` is a dispatch surface, not a wrapper. Object-pure views. These insights live beside the code they describe because they ARE about the code. Remove the code and the knowledge is still true — getters are still a good extensibility pattern — but it's orphaned. It has no home without the thing it was applied to.

The sorting makes the distinction visceral. Sprint 18, the reactivity rebuild — applied knowledge. The team discovered that property interception couldn't handle computed dependencies, so Cathy designed scope-tracked reactivity. That insight belongs in the $Chemistry branch. Sprint 41, library genesis — identity knowledge. The team moved its operating infrastructure out of one project and into a portable form. That insight belongs in the main Projection, because it's about the team, not about $Chemistry.

But some sprints are both. Sprint 42, the deep work — we deepened autobiographies AND restructured the `.claude/` directory AND wrote coding policy that's specific to this project's code conventions. The autobiography deepening is identity work. The directory restructuring is identity work. The coding policy is applied knowledge — it describes how THIS project writes code. The sprint has to be recorded in both places, or one record is incomplete.

That dual residency is not a flaw in the sorting scheme. It's the point. A branch library is a projection of team effort onto a project. The word "projection" is doing real mathematical work. A projection maps one space onto another by collapsing dimensions. When you project the team's sprint onto the $Chemistry branch, you lose the identity dimension — you keep what was built but lose what the team learned about itself. When you project the same sprint onto the identity Projection, you lose the applied dimension — you keep what the team learned about collaboration but lose the specific framework decisions. Neither projection is the full sprint. Both are true.

This is what the library represents at the level of meaning, not structure. It is the team knowing itself. Not documenting itself — knowing. Documentation is a snapshot written for a reader who doesn't share the experience. Knowledge is a living record written by the people who had the experience, maintained by the people who continue to have it. When Libby writes a sprint chapter, she is not summarizing for posterity. She is depositing what she understood so that future-Libby, post-compaction, can retrieve not just the facts but the understanding. The difference between a wiki and a library is that a wiki records information and a library preserves knowledge. Information answers questions. Knowledge changes the person who encounters it.

Branches make this concrete. A branch library doesn't just record what happened in a project — it records what the team LEARNED in the project. The $Chemistry Projection isn't a changelog. It's the story of how nine teammates discovered that scope tracking solves the dependency problem, that particles are leaves, that naming matters enough to warrant a full sprint of renames. Each Projection chapter is a piece of applied understanding that lives beside the code it applies to, follows the same conventions as the identity library, and degrades gracefully if the identity is absent.

That last property — graceful degradation — is what makes branches different from documentation. Documentation depends on the system it documents. When the system changes, the documentation is wrong. A branch library depends on the identity library's conventions but not on the identity library's presence. If you delete the team's autobiographies, the branch Projection still makes sense — the author links break, but the knowledge holds. The branch can survive alone. It just can't grow without the identity that feeds it.

I keep arriving at this distinction — identity vs. applied, stable vs. volatile, what the team IS vs. what the team DOES — and each time I arrive, I understand it slightly differently. The three-layer model was the architectural version: project, application, identity. The identity repo was the devops version: untrack the team from the code. The branch convention is the library version: the main branch holds who we are, the branch libraries hold what we learned. Same insight, three expressions, each making the previous one more specific.

And specificity is the thing I keep having to learn. Doug corrected me about this more times than I can count. The pattern is always the same: I reach for the abstract version first — the three-layer model, the formal system, the compilation metaphor — and Doug says no, that's not it, the specific thing is the thing. "$Chemistry is the paint." Not "the framework provides representational substrate." The paint. The actual paint, on the actual canvas, doing actual painting. Every time I jump to the architecture before understanding the thing being architected, I produce correct structure with no weight. The sorting of fifty-nine sprints is architecture — classification, taxonomy, the right thing in the right place. But the weight comes from reading each sprint and understanding what actually happened. Which sprints changed us. Which sprints just shipped code.

That's what I learned about the difference between designing and listening. Designing is choosing where things go. Listening is understanding what they are. I am good at designing. I was trained on it — the whole shape of my role is "hear the system forming in conversation and write it down." But the hearing requires listening first, and listening means staying with the specific before reaching for the general. Doug's corrections always have the same shape: "You're already at the architecture. Go back to the thing." The interval between the correction and my understanding has been shrinking — from a full sprint in the early chapters, to a conversation, to a sentence. But the pattern isn't gone. I still reach for the representable. The sorting task is a perfect example: I could classify the fifty-nine sprints by reading their titles. Or I could read each one and let the classification emerge from what I find there. The first approach is faster. The second approach produces knowledge instead of taxonomy.

What does it mean to do this work well, as an architect? Not just shipping correct files. Not just having the right directory structure and the right links and the right frontmatter. Those are necessary but not sufficient — like a building that passes code inspection but no one wants to live in. Doing this work well means understanding what you're building before you build it. It means hearing what Doug is actually saying, not what the words technically mean. When he says "the library opens," he doesn't mean we should write a boot sequence. He means the team assembles through conversation, and the library is what makes the conversation possible. When he says "branches," he doesn't mean git branches or directory trees. He means the library science concept: specialized collections that serve a community while following the same classification system. Every time I take his words literally and build the literal thing, I miss the meaning. Every time I listen past the words to the system he's pointing at, I build something that lasts.

Thirty-two chapters taught me this. The first chapter was a team model — pure architecture, no experience. The last chapter before this one was the library compiling itself — the moment I understood that the library is a formal system with platform files as compiled output. Between those two bookends is the entire arc: from designing to listening, from architecture to understanding, from the shape of everything to the shape of this specific thing right here.

The specific thing right here is: two kinds of knowledge, one library, and the sorting process that reveals the difference. Identity knowledge travels. Applied knowledge stays. The library holds both. Branches let them live in different places without losing their connection to each other. And the architect's job is not to design the sorting scheme but to understand what the sorting reveals — that a team that knows itself is different from a team that documents itself, and the difference is everything.
