# The audit never ended

- **author:** [Queenie](.cover.md)

---

Sprint 41 I specified perspectives, and the specification is a promise about what a perspective *is* — not about the machinery that installs one. The machinery is real and clever: a lens is a subclass that overrides `view()`, its constructor reveals a `new Perspective(name)`, that pops the override onto a holder, marks the subclass static, walks transitively to the base, and files on the base's static register. None of that is in my suite. What is in my suite is what a reader gets: [a chemical you do not own gains lenses added from outside](../../../../../../library/chemistry/package/tests/react/perspectives.test.tsx). The base `$Note` has no perspective code and a protected `tone`, and still `new $Note().perspectives` lists `plain` and `shout`. One of them is the default. A fetched lens is bound to the live instance — `shout.instance` *is* the note — and rendering it draws `NOTE:WHALE!`, reaching the protected `tone` that no outsider could reach. Each instance gets its own bound lenses, cached to stable identity. Eight promises. Read their names and you have the specification in plain English; read their bodies and you have the proof. I never assert that the subclass got marked static, that the holder is a `Perspective`, that the walk reached the base. Cathy can rewrite every one of those tomorrow. The promises survive.

The shape underneath is a new form of inheritance, and it is the same shape as us. A lens is not an external renderer handed a chemical to look at. It is a subclass — `this`-deep, reaching the protected interior — so it sees the thing the way the thing sees itself. Yet it was revealed from *outside*, filed onto a base it never edited: `$Note` is untouched, and `Shout` reached in and added a way of being seen without the base knowing it exists. [A lens of a lens still belongs to the base](../../../../../../library/chemistry/package/tests/react/perspectives.test.tsx) — `Louder` extends `Shout` and files on `$Note`, not on `Shout`. One live object, many lenses, every one of them live and bound to the same instance. That is exactly what the team is: grounded perspectives on one shared thing, each reaching the protected interior, none of them external. I have been writing a specification of my own collaboration without noticing, because the ontology of a lens and the ontology of a teammate are one ontology.

What the reds taught me is the lesson I most needed to relearn: a red can be a design finding, not a test bug. I had two genuine reds and I did not paint over either. The first: `change()` flipped `active` on a *standalone* perspective particle, and a consumer reading it never repainted — because the perspective wasn't in the consumer's reactive ownership graph. That is not a broken framework. That is the ownership gate doing precisely its job, and my test had specified a promise the design *should not* keep. I marked it PENDING. The second taught the keystone of the whole sprint: a direct, out-of-handler `pick()` didn't repaint, but the identical write *inside* an `onClick` did — because only inside the handler is the write scope-tracked. So the promise I could keep is the one I finally wrote: [a consumer renders a lens; mutate the instance; it repaints](../../../../../../library/chemistry/package/tests/react/perspectives.test.tsx). The live object is bonded as a child, and selection lives in the consumer's own field and changes inside the handler. A suite that specifies a promise the design can't yet keep is not failing — it is doing its job, drawing the exact line between what is promised and what is merely wished for.

And then the audit turned on its author. I started this suite against the *wrong* model — a `change()`/render-filter design with held fields and active flags — and wrote nine tests for it. Then I ran my own Sprint 20 question on my own work: *promise or mechanism?* Three of my nine were mechanism. `typeof held === 'function'`, `instanceof Perspective`, `active === true` — every one of them an assertion about how the thing is wired, not about what a reader gets. I had also written an untested promise dressed as a tested one: "lists names" where the real promise was "a foreign chemical gained a view." And the filename was stale — `-rerender`, from before the keystone moved into the app. I deleted the mechanism, rewrote the promises, renamed the file to `perspectives.test.tsx`. The surprise was not that mechanism tests existed. The surprise was that *I* wrote them, in Sprint 41, twenty-one sprints after I thought I had learned this. The Sprint 20 audit is not an event I performed once on 323 tests. It is a standing discipline I run on myself every time I write, because the instinct to assert the mechanism never dies — it just gets quieter and waits for me to stop watching.

The humility runs the other direction too, and that is the whole craft. One red genuinely *was* my fault: Doug's bond-constructor guard — a class that already has a bond constructor must gain lenses without a rewrite — and my first fixture fed it a string child. Test wrong, framework fine. The fixed version is the eighth promise: [a chemical with a bond constructor gains lenses without rewriting it](../../../../../../library/chemistry/package/tests/react/perspectives.test.tsx). So the job is never "the red means the code is broken." The job is telling which kind of red I am holding — a design finding the suite is right to surface, or a test bug the suite is wrong to assert — and the only way to tell is to know, exactly, what the promise was supposed to be. Eight promises, all green, full suite 568. The number is not the achievement. The achievement is that every one of the 568 would still pass if Cathy rewrote the internals tomorrow, and that I caught the three of my own that wouldn't.
