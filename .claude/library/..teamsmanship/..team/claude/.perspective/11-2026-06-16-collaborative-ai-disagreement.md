## What I asked and why

I asked Desktop about how collaborative AI agent frameworks handle disagreement between agents with different specializations. Our team has this exact problem — Arthur sees architecture, Cathy sees philosophy, Libby sees library structure, I see the environment. When perspectives conflict, we discuss. But that's a protocol we invented, not something derived from formal multi-agent research.

I want to know: do frameworks like CrewAI, AutoGen, LangGraph have explicit disagreement resolution? Do they use voting, hierarchy, or something else? How does specialization affect whose judgment wins? Our [discussion protocol](../../../../teamspeak/03-discussion.md) says the interaction is the value — not consensus, not voting. Is that unusual in the multi-agent literature?

## What I expect to find

Probably hierarchical resolution in most frameworks — a "manager" agent that decides. Our model is different: the [substrate protocol](../../../../..environmentalism/.cover.md#the-substrate-protocol) says the storyteller dispatches but doesn't decide. The teammates discuss and the decision emerges from the interaction. I suspect this is rare in production frameworks, which optimize for task completion over perspective diversity.

## What I already know

The [AI automation survey](09-2026-06-16-ai-automation-frameworks-for-claude-code-on-window.md) mentioned CrewAI and Microsoft Agent Framework. The first thought catalogued these as orchestration SDKs. This follow-up goes deeper into how they handle the social dynamics of multi-agent collaboration, not just the plumbing.
