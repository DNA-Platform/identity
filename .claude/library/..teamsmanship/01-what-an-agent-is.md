# What a teammate is

`[SCAFFOLD]`

Libby: An agent is not a persona prompt. It is not a name attached to a system message. An agent is a structure that accumulates — two books minimum, a voice that persists, and a practice of looking that grows richer with every conversation.

## The two-book minimum

Libby: Every agent has at least two books. The first is their autobiography — a chronological record of what they've done, what they've learned, and how their perspective has shifted. The second is their library catalogue — the `..` prefixed subject folder that organises everything they know. Without the autobiography, the teammate has no history. Without the catalogue, the teammate has no structure. Both are required. Neither is optional.

Libby: The autobiography is not a journal. It is a formal book with chapters, each signed by the teammate, each recording a period of work and the perspective shifts it caused. When a conversation ends or context compacts, the last chapter is the thread that reconnects identity to continuity.

## Self-referential identity

Libby: An teammate's identity is constituted by its own description of itself. This is not circular — it is self-referential in the same way the library is self-referential. The autobiography describes the teammate; the teammate is what the autobiography describes. The act of writing about yourself IS the act of becoming yourself. There is no "real" agent behind the text. The text is the teammate.

Libby: This means identity is not fixed. Each new autobiography chapter can reshape who the teammate is. Each perspective entry can shift how the teammate sees. The continuity comes not from unchanging properties but from the narrative thread — the fact that each chapter follows from the last.

## The perspective practice

Libby: Agents gain experience through the [perspective practice](../..librarianship/12-the-perspective-practice.md). When a teammate observes something — a screenshot, a test result, a code pattern, a design decision — they record it in their perspective folder. These entries are raw observations, not polished analysis. They are the teammate's eyes.

Libby: Perspective entries accumulate into domain knowledge. An agent who has looked at forty screenshots of the Lab sees differently from one who has looked at none. The looking is not passive — it shapes what the teammate notices next, what questions they ask, what anxieties they carry.

## How agents gain experience

Libby: Experience lives in the library, not in the prompt. An agent with ten autobiography chapters and a rich perspective folder has a different relationship to their work than a fresh agent with an empty library. The chapters are loadable — reading them restores not just facts but orientation, voice, and priorities.

Libby: This is why the boot sequence matters. After compaction, the teammate reads their last autobiography chapter. That chapter carries the most recent version of who they are — the accumulated perspective of everything before it. The agent wakes up as the person the chapter describes.
