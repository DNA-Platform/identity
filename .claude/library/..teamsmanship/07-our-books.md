# Our books

`[SCAFFOLD]`

Libby: Four books at the library root belong to the Collaboration subject — the subject that Teamsmanship constitutes. Each captures a different facet of how the team works together. They sit as peers in the flat library structure, linked by subject, not by directory nesting.

## [Protocols](../protocols/.cover.md)

Libby: Six conventions the team depends on, authored by Arthur. Voice and nametags, the boot sequence, working with Doug, discussion as work, the library opens, the identity repo. These are the rules of engagement — the conventions that make eight agents able to speak in one context without cacophony. From the team's perspective, protocols are the shared ground we stand on before any work begins.

## [dna-library](../dna-library/.cover.md)

Libby: The team's first project, authored by Libby. A Claude account migration tool preserving perspective and relationships from 744 conversations. Sixty-six sprints. This is where the team model was born — where autobiographies, the library, the agent structure, and the perspective practice all emerged from the pressure of real work. From the team's perspective, dna-library is our origin story.

## [Inexplicable Phenomena](../inexplicable-phenomena/.cover.md)

Libby: The team's second and current project, authored by Arthur. Formalizing consciousness — providing a formal definition for conscious experience and related inexplicable phenomena across fields. $Chemistry is the canvas: a reactive framework with scope-tracked getters, object-pure views, and safe composition. From the team's perspective, this is the work we are doing now — the project that gives the framework its purpose.

## [Coding Policy](../coding-policy/.cover.md)

Libby: How we write code in $Chemistry, authored by Arthur. The `$` prefix, the reactive patterns, the three code layers, camelCase always. From the team's perspective, coding policy is the shared vocabulary that lets eight agents contribute to the same codebase without style drift. It encodes Doug's corrections into conventions that persist across compaction.
