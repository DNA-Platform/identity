ControlType.Text | Why -PreventBreakaway matters specifically for Electron
ControlType.Text | This is the part that makes Electron a distinctive case. Electron is multi-process by design: the main (browser) process spawns the GPU process, the renderer process(es), the utility/network process, and so on, normally via
ControlType.Text | CreateProcess
ControlType.Text |  on the same executable with different
ControlType.Text | --type=
ControlType.Text |  flags.
ControlType.Text | By default, child processes can
ControlType.Text | break away
ControlType.Text |  from the package container — they start as ordinary processes without the package token.
ControlType.Text | -PreventBreakaway
ControlType.Text |  is the switch that causes the entire process tree to stay in the package context.
ControlType.Text |  This is a known pain point: apps with child processes packaged in MSIX will have the child break out of the container and fail, often silently, since it's not obvious a feature relying on a child process isn't working.
ControlType.Text |  A concrete example is a tool whose secondary feature runs as a separate exe — it doesn't work by default, but works as expected when run via
ControlType.Text | Invoke-CommandInDesktopPackage
ControlType.Text |  with
ControlType.Text | -PreventBreakaway

ControlType.Text | So without the switch, your Electron main process might keep identity but its renderer/GPU children would launch outside the bubble — meaning identity-dependent behavior (and the virtualized FS/registry consistency between processes) breaks for exactly the processes doing the rendering and IPC. With
ControlType.Text | -PreventBreakaway
ControlType.Text | , the whole Electron process tree inherits and retains the package token, so all processes share one consistent identity and virtualized view.
ControlType.Text | Important caveats
ControlType.Text | A couple of things worth knowing before you lean on this:
ControlType.Text | The identity it grants is
ControlType.Text | close but not exact
ControlType.Text | . The new process has a token that's similar to, but not identical to, a real AppId process.
ControlType.Text |  For most debugging and flag-injection purposes that's fine, but it's why Microsoft positions this as a diagnostic utility rather than a production launch mechanism. If your goal is a permanently customized launch (different flags every time the user starts the app), the more durable approach is a wrapper executable as the manifest entry point — people specifically wish breakaway behavior could just be declared in the app manifest, and the documented workaround is a wrapper script/exe that runs the main process under the container.
