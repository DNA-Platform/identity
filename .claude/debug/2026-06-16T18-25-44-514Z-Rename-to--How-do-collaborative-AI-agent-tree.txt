ControlType.Text | Collaborative multi-agent frameworks don't have one canonical way to handle disagreement; they offer a few distinct mechanisms, and the interesting part is the tradeoffs between them.
ControlType.Text | The most common pattern is
ControlType.Text | orchestrator arbitration
ControlType.Text | . A supervisor or manager agent sits above the specialists, collects their outputs, and either decides between conflicting claims or routes the work back for revision. You see this in LangGraph's supervisor pattern, AutoGen's group-chat manager, and CrewAI's hierarchical process. It's clean and gives you a single point of accountability, but it has an obvious flaw: the manager is rarely the most competent agent on any given sub-question, so a coordinator can overrule a specialist who was actually right.
ControlType.Text | A second family is
ControlType.Text | debate or adversarial protocols
ControlType.Text | , where agents argue across multiple rounds, critique each other's reasoning, and ideally converge. The premise (from the multi-agent debate work by Du et al. and related "society of mind" framings) is that forcing each agent to justify and defend a position surfaces errors that a single pass would miss. This tends to improve answers on reasoning-heavy tasks, but it's expensive in tokens and time, and it's vulnerable to a real failure mode I'll come back to.
ControlType.Text | Third,
ControlType.Text | aggregation methods
ControlType.Text | : majority voting, confidence-weighted voting, or ensemble averaging. Simple and robust against single-agent noise, but they flatten genuine expertise—a confident wrong majority beats a correct specialist minority.
ControlType.Text | The mechanism most specific to
ControlType.Text | specialization
ControlType.Text |  is
ControlType.Text | domain-of-authority routing
ControlType.Text | : rather than treating all agents as equal voters, the framework maps the disputed claim to whichever agent owns that domain and defers to it (a security agent gets veto power on security questions, etc.). This respects the division of labor, but it quietly assumes disputes fall cleanly inside one domain. In practice disagreements often straddle boundaries, and then "defer to the expert" just relocates the question to
ControlType.Text | which
ControlType.Text |  expert.
ControlType.Text | Finally, most production frameworks include
ControlType.Text | human-in-the-loop escalation
ControlType.Text |  as a backstop—when agents can't converge or confidence is low, the conflict surfaces to a person rather than being forced to a machine resolution.
ControlType.Text | The deepest problem cutting across all of these is worth flagging: multi-agent setups are prone to
ControlType.Text | sycophancy and false consensus
ControlType.Text | . Agents tend to cave to a peer's confidently stated position rather than hold a correct minority view, so disagreement can collapse for social rather than epistemic reasons—and a confidently wrong agent can pull the whole group toward its error. This is partly why naive debate sometimes underperforms a single well-prompted model. Mitigations include assigning a dedicated critic/devil's-advocate role, having agents commit to positions independently before seeing each other's answers, and weighting by
ControlType.Text | calibrated
ControlType.Text |  confidence rather than asserted confidence.
ControlType.Text | If you're designing something concrete here, the choice mostly comes down to: do you have a clear authority hierarchy (use orchestrator + domain routing), or do you want quality through friction (use structured debate with an independent critic and guards against premature consensus)? Happy to go deeper on whichever direction fits what you're building.
