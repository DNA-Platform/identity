ControlType.Text | The split comes down to one being a raw transport and the other a full RPC framework, and on Windows specifically the transport story has a wrinkle worth knowing about.
ControlType.Text | Named pipes
ControlType.Text |  are a genuine local IPC primitive. On the .NET side you get
ControlType.Text | NamedPipeServerStream
ControlType.Text | /
ControlType.Text | NamedPipeClientStream
ControlType.Text |  (
ControlType.Text | System.IO.Pipes
ControlType.Text | ), and Node talks to the same
ControlType.Text | \\.\pipe\name
ControlType.Text |  path through the
ControlType.Text | net
ControlType.Text |  module (
ControlType.Text | net.createServer
ControlType.Text | /
ControlType.Text | net.connect
ControlType.Text |  accept a pipe path directly). What you get is a bidirectional byte stream — kernel-managed, no TCP stack, no port to allocate, no firewall prompt. Everything above the bytes is yours to build: serialization (JSON, MessagePack, protobuf-by-hand) and framing (length prefixes or delimiters, since the stream won't preserve message boundaries for you).
ControlType.Text | gRPC
ControlType.Text |  is schema-first: you write a
ControlType.Text | .proto
ControlType.Text | , generate typed stubs for both
ControlType.Text | @grpc/grpc-js
ControlType.Text |  and .NET (
ControlType.Text | Grpc.Net.Client
ControlType.Text |  /
ControlType.Text | Grpc.AspNetCore
ControlType.Text | ), and get request/response semantics, deadlines, cancellation, metadata, status codes, and all four streaming modes for free. The contract is the thing both sides compile against, and protobuf is built for forward/backward compatibility as the schema evolves.
ControlType.Text | The Windows wrinkle: gRPC runs over HTTP/2, which normally means TCP. .NET 8+ Kestrel can actually listen on a named pipe endpoint, so a .NET↔.NET setup can get gRPC semantics over a pipe transport. But
ControlType.Text | grpc-js
ControlType.Text |  on the Node side has no named-pipe transport and only shaky AF_UNIX support on Windows. So in practice,
ControlType.Text | Node↔.NET gRPC on Windows means localhost TCP
ControlType.Text |  — which reintroduces a port, the TCP/IP stack, and potential firewall involvement that named pipes sidestep entirely.
ControlType.Text | That difference drives most of the practical trade-offs:
ControlType.ListItem | Security. Named pipes carry Windows ACLs — you can restrict access by SID or integrity level, a clean local-auth story. A gRPC TCP listener on loopback is reachable by any local process unless you layer on TLS/mTLS or token auth yourself.
ControlType.Text | Security.
ControlType.ListItem | Performance. For high-frequency small messages, raw pipes have lower overhead (no HTTP/2 framing). gRPC adds protocol overhead but protobuf is fast and HTTP/2 multiplexing handles concurrency well; for typical structured RPC workloads the overhead is rarely the bottleneck.
ControlType.Text | Performance.
ControlType.ListItem | Concurrency & connection management. gRPC handles multiple concurrent calls over one channel and manages reconnection/channel state. With pipes you manage multiple pipe instances and write your own reconnect logic.
ControlType.Text | Concurrency & connection management.
ControlType.ListItem | Setup cost. Pipes need nothing beyond the standard library on both sides. gRPC adds packages, a proto toolchain in your build, and generated code to manage — but pays you back with type safety, interceptors, reflection, and health checks.
ControlType.Text | Setup cost.
ControlType.Text | A rough heuristic: if it's strictly same-machine, you want tight Windows ACL control, the protocol is simple, and you'd rather not carry a build-time codegen step, named pipes are the lighter choice. If you want a typed contract across the two languages, real streaming semantics, and the option to move the .NET service off-box later without rewriting the wire protocol, gRPC earns its overhead — just go in expecting localhost TCP rather than a pipe transport on the Node end.
ControlType.Text | One pragmatic middle path some teams take: named pipes as the transport with protobuf (or JSON) as the payload and a small length-prefix framing layer. You keep the local-IPC benefits and a schema, without the HTTP/2 layer — at the cost of hand-building the RPC dispatch that gRPC would give you.
