ControlType.Text | Link consistency at scale breaks down into two fundamentally different problems that these systems solve in opposite ways:
ControlType.Text | internal links
ControlType.Text |  (page-to-page within the system) and
ControlType.Text | external links
ControlType.Text |  (out to the open web). The first is solvable with strong consistency guarantees; the second is fundamentally a best-effort, eventual-cleanup problem.
ControlType.Text | Internal links: the indexed-reference approach
ControlType.Text | The core insight in both MediaWiki and Confluence is to
ControlType.Text | not store links as raw URLs
ControlType.Text | . Instead, links are parsed at save time and recorded in a separate index that maps source page → target, so the system always knows the full backlink graph without crawling.
ControlType.Text | In MediaWiki this is the
ControlType.Text | pagelinks
ControlType.Text |  table. Each entry contains the source page's ID and a reference to the linktarget table which has the title and namespace that is being linked to from within that source page.
ControlType.Text |  A key design detail: links target a
ControlType.Text | namespace + title key
ControlType.Text | , not a page ID. The target page may or may not exist, and due to renames and deletions may refer to different page records as time goes by.
ControlType.Text |  This is why a redlink (link to a not-yet-created page) and a working link are stored identically — existence is resolved at render time by joining against the page table, not baked into the link.

ControlType.Hyperlink | MediaWiki
ControlType.Text | That same index powers the "What links here" backlink feature. The pages linking to and/or embedding a given page are listed, and this info comes from the pagelinks table and the templatelinks table.
ControlType.Text |  The interesting architectural decision in newer MediaWiki is the
ControlType.Text | linktarget
ControlType.Text |  table — an indirection layer where a page namespace-title tuple gets a unique integer ID, used for referenced page titles in templatelinks, pagelinks, and categorylinks.
ControlType.Text |  This means a single title is stored once and referenced by integer everywhere, which matters enormously when one popular template is transcluded across millions of pages.

ControlType.Text | The reason links are stored by
ControlType.Text | title-key
ControlType.Text |  rather than
ControlType.Text | page-ID
ControlType.Text |  drives how moves work. There are two competing strategies:
ControlType.Text | Strategy A — Title-keyed links + redirects (MediaWiki).
ControlType.Text |  When a page moves, its links don't update at all. The old title becomes a redirect record pointing to the new title, and the link resolution simply follows it. This is cheap (one redirect row, no rewrite of millions of referencing pages) but introduces redirect chains. MediaWiki has a dedicated background job and a maintenance report for
ControlType.Text | double redirects
ControlType.Text |  (A→B→C), which a bot flattens to A→C. The tradeoff: links technically still "work" but accumulate a layer of indirection, and "What links here" must account for links arriving via redirect.
ControlType.Text | Strategy B — ID-keyed links + link refactoring (Confluence).
ControlType.Text |  Confluence links resolve through internal page/content IDs rather than titles, so renaming a page leaves all inbound links intact automatically — the title is just a display label resolved from the ID. When a page is
ControlType.Text | moved
ControlType.Text |  (reparented or moved across spaces), Confluence runs "link refactoring" that rewrites the stored references so they continue to resolve. The cost is that the move operation is heavier (it touches the referencing content), but there's no redirect-chain accumulation and no ambiguity about whether a link is live.
ControlType.Text | Both approaches share the same guarantee that web-style links lack: because the system controls both ends and indexes them at write time, an internal link can
ControlType.Text | never silently rot
ControlType.Text | . The worst case is a redlink (clearly flagged) or a redirect (transparently followed).
